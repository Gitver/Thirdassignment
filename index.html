Oil prices: do they affect inflation?¶

Edgar Brouwer (430777), Nick Goedhart (405071) & Arjan Spaans (181637)

Research question¶

Low inflation in France: to what extent are low oil prices responsible?

Motivation¶

During the early 2000s until 2008 oil prices hit all-time heights, but when the financial crisis hit however, the price of oil declined sharply and also from last year until now many commodities have seen sharp declining prices. The inflation rate in the Eurozone has also fallen during the last three years. In the year 2012 the inflation was on the five-year average of about 2.5% while now it is around only around one percent. Since oil has seen its prices decrease at similar rates as the inflation, the question occurs whether the changes in oil prices affect the inflation rate. Answering this question and thus understanding the linkage between oil prices and inflation is not only vital for monetary policy makers for conducting sound monetary policies but also for firm managers since if oil prices affect the prices of production, prices of goods need to be changed as well. Additionally, most research regarding the effect of oil prices on inflation is outdated since it does not take into account the post-financial crisis price decreases. Most of the research is done for the US instead of the Eurozone and researchers typically are interested in price increases instead of decreases that we saw during the last few years.

Answer¶

Although it is striaghtforward to believe that oil prices have a direct and indirect effect on inflation we did not manage to find statistical evidence for this effect in the first model used.

In the second model which uses fewer observations we do find that if oil prices increase with 1%, inflation increases with about 0.4%

Further research using different models (for instance models that investigate a non-linear trend) is adviced. Also we did not consider time lags in our analysis what can be of great importance in time series analysis.

Method¶

In order to investigate this topic, we worked with time series data from 1987-2015 which we downloaded from the website of the French National Bank and OECD Stat. Although the main focus of our study is on the impact of oil price changes on inflation specifically, we included some control variables: unemployment rate (Unemp), broad money supply (M3) and differences in exchange rate (Dolleur). In order to assure that the variables were stationary, we used the log differences for the variables in numbers (CPI, M3 money supply and oil prices) while leaving the variables that are rates untouched. This leads to the following regression:

$Difinfl_t = \beta_1DifOil_t + \beta_2Interest_t + \beta_3Unemp_t + \beta_4Dolleur + \beta_5Difm3 + \epsilon_t$

We used the Pandas module in Python to analyse the data.

Assumptions¶

In order for this model to produce good estimates, the Gauss-Markov assumptions need to hold. These are listed here. For the purpose of this assignment, we only tested for stationarity using the Dickey-Fuller Test.

from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller
def test_stationarity(timeseries):
    
    #Lines for determination of rolling statistics
    rolmean = pd.rolling_mean(timeseries, window=12)
    rolstd = pd.rolling_std(timeseries, window=12)

    #Lines used for plotting rolling statistics:
    orig = plt.plot(timeseries, color='blue',label='Original')
    mean = plt.plot(rolmean, color='red', label='Rolling Mean')
    std = plt.plot(rolstd, color='black', label = 'Rolling Std')
    plt.legend(loc='best')
    plt.title('Rolling Mean & Standard Deviation')
    plt.show(block=False)
    
    #Lines used for performing the Dickey-Fuller test:
    print 'Results of Dickey-Fuller Test:'
    dftest = adfuller(timeseries, autolag='AIC')
    dfoutput = pd.Series(dftest[0:4], index=['Test Statistic','p-value','#Lags Used','Number of Observations Used'])
    for key,value in dftest[4].items():
        dfoutput['Critical Value (%s)'%key] = value
    print dfoutput

Conclusion & Discussion¶

Although it took some effort to finish this assignment we made it to the final cell of this notebook. Unfortunately, the model used in this assignment does not work very well for at least the first model used. Although the coefficient for Oil is positive it is far from significant. As can be expected, the most influential independent variable is the interest rate, what is also used by monetary policy makers for controling inflation rates.

In the second model we do confirm a positive and significant relationship where if the oil price increases by one percent, inflation increases by 0.4%. There is however some scope for improvement since in both models time lags could be considered since it is likely that it takes some time for changes in oil prices to affect inflation. It could also be worthwile taking a closer look to structural breaks.
